# Art-Department Craft — Props, Locations & Sets

This document is the *creative* companion to `guide-prop-turntable.md` and `guide-location-pack.md`. Where those guides address the technical problem of locking objects and environments across AI generations, this one addresses the art that precedes and informs it — how real propmasters and production designers build the physical world that a film inhabits. Each role is dual: a craft with its own rigorous discipline and a tool for translating director intent into a tangible, camera-ready environment. A Production Designer section will be added in a later phase to complete the department.

## Props — the object as character

An object placed in a story is never merely an object. It carries the weight of period, class, personality, and intention — and the camera, which has no choice but to register everything within the frame, will make the audience read it. The propmaster's discipline is the art of controlling that reading: deciding which objects the story needs to trust, which it needs to ignore, and which it needs to use, break, or destroy.

**Hero, dressing, and action props.** The prop department divides its inventory into three classes that carry very different production consequences. *Hero props* are objects that appear in close-up, are handled extensively by cast, and must be finished to photographic quality — every surface, material, and detail will be inspected by the camera at some point. *Set-dressing props* populate the environment: they establish period and world without the camera ever dwelling on them. *Action props* are objects scripted to be used, opened, fired, operated, or destroyed; they often require mechanical modification, safety certification, or working versions alongside visual-only stand-ins. Conflating these classes in pre-production leads to either over-engineered dressing props or, worse, under-finished hero pieces that fail in close-up.

**The prop that tells story.** A well-chosen prop communicates status, period, psychology, and narrative history in a single image. A monogrammed cigarette case places its owner in a class and era; a cracked photograph establishes absence and grief; a briefcase that glows when opened must withhold its contents from the audience while registering them as irresistible. The propmaster reads the script not for what objects are mentioned but for what they mean — what a character's relationship to each object reveals about who they are and where they have been.

**Multiples and the math of destruction.** Any prop that must be handled extensively, broken, dropped, or operated across many takes exists not as a single object but as a family of identical or near-identical duplicates. A hero prop built in fives may be shot from multiple angles simultaneously, worn at different rates by cast, and destroyed in practical sequences — all while appearing continuous to the audience. The fabrication budget must account for the full multiple count before production begins; discovering mid-shoot that two of three hero items are gone and no duplicates remain is a production emergency that planning prevents.

**State variants as narrative.** A prop's condition is itself a form of storytelling. The propmaster often maintains several state versions of a key object: pristine, lightly worn, heavily distressed, damaged, and destroyed. Each state corresponds to a story moment, and moving between them is a continuity discipline as demanding as any other department's. Blood, mud, water damage, and fire char must be reproduced exactly across shooting days that may be separated by weeks; aging and breakdown are applied incrementally and documented in the continuity record.

**Fabrication and sourcing realities.** Not every prop can be sourced; not every prop should be fabricated. The propmaster navigates a range of options — prop-house rental, period purchase, modern manufacture dressed to period, and bespoke construction — and the choice carries budget and schedule consequences. Period props rented from established houses may be unavailable in sufficient multiples; bespoke fabrication guarantees uniqueness but requires lead time, craft expertise, and material knowledge. Foam, fiberglass, resin casting, vacuum-forming, metalwork, and upholstery are all live options, and a working propmaster must know when each is appropriate.

**The propmaster's continuity discipline.** The propmaster maintains a continuity record as demanding as the script supervisor's — photographs, sketches, and written notes documenting every object's exact state, position, and orientation for each scene. An object that moves between takes, that is consumed (food, cigarettes, breakables) without identical replacement, or that changes state without documented reason creates a continuity error that editors cannot cut around. The discipline is relentless precisely because most of it is invisible when executed correctly.

## Locations & Sets — environment as storytelling

The environment in which a story unfolds is not backdrop — it is argument. A location or a set expresses the logic of the world before any character speaks: the height of ceilings, the quality of light through particular windows, the degree of order or disorder in the arrangement of objects. Production design is the art of making those expressions precise and intentional. The decisions made in pre-production — during the recce or the drafting phase — constrain or liberate every department that follows.

**The scouting packet and the recce beyond the frame.** A location scout does not seek a picturesque backdrop; it seeks a workable premise. The scouting packet — location photographs, ground plans, access information, sun-path data, and notes on ambient sound and neighboring structures — is a production document as functional as a budget. The recce reveals what the camera will see in every direction, what construction or dressing will be needed to close the frame, and what logistical realities will shape the shooting day. A location approved on the basis of a single hero angle that ignores the reverse is a mistake that will cost time and money on set.

**Environment carries disproportionate story.** Where characters exist shapes who they are allowed to be. A low-ceilinged, cluttered room makes different demands on the body than a vaulted institutional space; a location saturated with historical detail requires different performance energy than one that reads as contemporary or abstract. The production designer uses space deliberately as a pressure on character — designing environments that restrict, liberate, intimidate, or comfort according to what the narrative requires at each moment.

**Sun-path and the logic of light.** Every location is governed by where the sun rises and sets, at what angle, and at what hour. A room with windows facing west can only receive direct golden light in the late afternoon; a location chosen for its morning quality will be flat and uninteresting if the schedule demands a midday shoot. The production designer and director of photography solve this together, but the solution begins at the location decision: north-facing studios offer consistent diffuse light; south-facing interiors offer controlled directional light. On location, the sun-path determines which rooms are usable at which hours — and that determination shapes the entire shooting schedule.

**Reverse-angle coherence and set extension.** In a built set, the production designer controls every surface the camera can see — and must account for the reverse angle as rigorously as the primary one. A set that is richly designed on camera right but unfinished on camera left is a trap: a director who decides mid-shoot to play a scene in the other direction will find nothing to cut to. Set extension — practical construction supplemented by digital or physical continuation of the environment — requires planning at the design stage. What the art department calls "closing the room" (ensuring every direction is shootable) is among the most under-budgeted items in pre-production.

**Location dressing, built sets, and set decoration.** The production designer works across three distinct modes of environment-making. A *found location* is dressed by the set decorator — objects added, removed, or repositioned to serve the story while working within the architectural reality of the existing space. A *built set* gives the production designer full control over every surface, angle, and dimension, at significantly higher cost. *Set decoration* is the discipline that populates either context: selecting, sourcing, aging, and arranging objects so that the world feels inhabited rather than assembled. The set decorator is the interior designer of the story, and their collaboration with the production designer determines whether an environment reads as true.

## Production design — orchestrating the visual world

The production designer is the head of the art department who holds Props and Locations & Sets under a single creative brief, ensuring that objects and environments speak to each other as parts of a unified visual argument. This section will be expanded in a later phase to cover the production designer's primary workflow: developing a concept from the script, building the visual hierarchy, coordinating with the cinematographer and director, and translating creative intent into the built and found environments the camera inhabits.

## How this feeds the pipeline

The art-department craft described here is the upstream creative foundation for two distinct technical workflows downstream. The principles of hero classification, multiples, and state variants feed directly into the `prop-turntable` workflow: the hero anchor — the pristine hero prop photographed in full ortho ring — is the reference asset that locks object identity across AI generations, and state variant descriptions define the generation variants the turntable must account for. The principles of the scouting packet, reverse-angle coherence, and set extension feed into the `location-pack` workflow: the master plate establishes the primary camera position, coverage variants encode the reverse and supplementary angles, and the lighting note captures the sun-path logic that determines which plates are consistent with which times of day.

The art department's most important contribution to the pipeline is not the reference asset itself but the vocabulary of questions that precede it — is this a hero prop or set dressing, which state variants does this scene require, is this a found location or a built set, and how closed is the room? These are the exact questions a propmaster or production designer would ask, and their answers determine what reference must be locked before generation begins. An agent that knows to ask them produces generation-ready briefs rather than generation-ready guesses.

Creative intent survives into a reference asset only when it has been made explicit and physical. A prop instinct left as "it should feel ancient and used" will drift; translated into material specifics — worn brass fittings, a replaced leather wrap in a lighter tone, a dent from a specific narrative event — it becomes a description that holds across generations. The same principle applies to location: "somewhere industrial and forgotten" is an aesthetic direction, not a generation anchor. The `location-pack` disciplines that into a plate with a confirmed sun angle, a dressing note, and a weather variant — elements the craft traditions in this file have always considered non-negotiable.
