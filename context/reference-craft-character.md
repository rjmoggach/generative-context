# Character Craft — Casting, Costume, Makeup & Hair

This document is the *creative* companion to `guide-character-consistency.md` and `guide-turnaround-sheets.md`. Where those guides address the technical problem of locking identity across AI generations, this one addresses the art that precedes and informs it — how real casting directors, costume designers, and makeup-and-hair artists build a character from the outside in. Each role is dual: it is both a craft with its own rigorous discipline and a tool for translating director intent into a living image.

---

## Casting — presence, typage, chemistry

A character is first understood through the body and face that inhabits it. Before a word of dialogue is spoken, the audience has already begun to read gender, age, class, threat level, and warmth — all of it registered in the first seconds of a performer's appearance on screen. Casting is the decision that sets every one of those registers.

**The face tells a story.** Every face carries a history — bone structure, weathering, the habitual expressions that have carved themselves into the flesh over years. A casting director looks for a face whose history is legible, one that the camera can read. The choice of face is, in narrative terms, a form of shorthand: it tells the audience who this person has been before the story began.

**Typage versus casting against type.** Classical cinema inherited from Eisenstein and the Soviet montage tradition the idea of *typage* — selecting performers for their physiognomic fit to a social role (the bourgeois, the soldier, the worker). Hollywood refined this into star personas, where the actor's known screen identity did much of the characterization. Casting against type is the conscious inversion: placing a beloved star in a morally contaminated role, or a physically unlikely actor in a role that convention would assign differently. Both moves are legible only because the baseline expectation exists.

**Presence over looks.** Screen magnetism is not beauty — it is a quality of concentration and aliveness that registers differently on camera than in life. Some performers are larger on screen than in a room; others shrink. The casting director's skill is identifying who the camera loves, and this judgment overrides conventional notions of attractiveness. Watchability — the quality that makes it impossible not to look at a performer when they are in frame — is the primary criterion.

**Ensemble chemistry and contrast.** Casting a single role is incomplete without considering the whole. Families must feel credibly related without being identical; antagonists must be plausibly matched or interestingly mismatched with protagonists; ensembles must contain enough range of type, coloring, and energy that the audience can track individuals within a crowd. Chemistry between performers is partly discovered in audition and partly constructed through contrast — the casting director builds a group, not just a list.

**What a casting director reads for.** In the room, the work is listening — not only to how a performer executes the material, but to whether they are genuinely present, whether they hear what is said to them, whether they surprise. The audition tests not performance quality in isolation but responsiveness: can this person take direction, shift register, inhabit uncertainty? Instinct matters because the camera records what the actor *is*, not only what they are doing.

## Costume — wardrobe as character

A character's clothing is not decoration applied after the character is defined — it is one of the primary means by which the character is defined. The costume designer reads the script not for plot but for psychology: what does this person want the world to think of them, what do they reveal against their will, and how does their self-image change across the story? The answers appear in silhouette, fabric, color, fit, and wear.

**Silhouette first.** Before a frame can read texture, pattern, or color, it reads shape. The silhouette of a costume — the line it makes against background and against other figures — is the first unit of visual communication. Wide-shouldered power tailoring flattens into authority; draped or shapeless garments suggest passivity or disguise; sharp cut reads precision and control. The costume designer builds silhouette before choosing cloth.

**Colour as character.** Costume and color story are inseparable — a character's wardrobe palette is one of the most consistent carriers of their associative hue across the cut. (See `guide-color-story.md`, section on associative color: hue tied to a character or theme.) The costume designer coordinates with the production designer and director of photography to ensure a character's clothes remain legible against their environment — not camouflaged by it unless that is the dramatic intent.

**Status, period, and era legibility.** A costume must locate the wearer in time, class, and culture within the first seconds of screen appearance. Period accuracy serves this function, but so does deliberate period distortion: a character who dresses anachronistically within their world is making a statement. The designer controls how much the audience must "work" to place a character — excessive legibility flattens; insufficient legibility alienates.

**The costume arc.** In long-form work, wardrobe is an arc, not a single decision. A character's clothes can begin pristine and become progressively worn; they can shift from muted to vivid as confidence grows; they can revert to an earlier palette as the character regresses. The designer tracks these changes across the whole story, treating wardrobe as a continuous thread of characterization.

**Distressing and aging as narrative.** A garment that has been lived in carries different meaning from a new one. Breakdown — fraying, fading, staining, mending — is a form of storytelling. Distressing is the craft of manufacturing the appearance of this history: bleaching, sanding, painting, controlled tearing. When done well it is invisible to the audience while registering subliminally as truth.

**Costume versus fashion.** Fashion is concerned with the garment; costume is concerned with the character. The two can coincide — a character defined by their fashion consciousness wears fashion — but the designer's loyalty is always to the role. Over-designed costumes announce themselves; the work succeeds when audiences remember the character, not the clothes.

## Makeup & Hair — the invisible craft

The highest praise for a makeup-and-hair department is that no one notices their work. Unlike costume, which is selected before filming, makeup and hair are applied to a living body every morning of the shoot — and must be reproduced to within a fraction of a millimetre across a production that may span months. The craft is simultaneously artistic and scientific: it demands skill in painting, sculpture, chemistry, anatomy, and above all an obsessive continuity discipline.

**The "no-makeup" look.** Naturalistic makeup — skin that reads as bare, eyes that simply read as eyes — is among the most technically demanding work in the department. It requires correcting asymmetry, evening tone, controlling reflectivity for the specific light and camera, and maintaining this across a full shooting day. The paradox is that the audience should believe they are seeing an unmade-up face while the makeup artist has made dozens of decisions to arrive at that impression.

**Aging and its anatomy.** Believable screen aging is not a single effect but a system — loss of skin elasticity produces specific textures (crepe, jowl, under-eye sag); fat redistribution changes facial contour; hair thins, grays, and shifts at the temples first; posture changes. Each element must be anatomically grounded: audiences may not be able to articulate what is wrong with implausible aging, but they register it as uncanny. The makeup artist works from photographic reference and anatomical knowledge, not from a generalized idea of "looking old."

**Wounds, SFX, and prosthetics as storytelling.** A prosthetic scar, a bruise that progresses across the story, a wound that refuses to heal — practical effects of this kind carry narrative weight that digital compositing rarely replicates convincingly in close-up. The texture of a prosthetic under raking light, the way a laceration stretches when the actor moves: these physical details land differently than their VFX equivalents. Foam latex and silicone prosthetics remain indispensable in productions that need the camera to get close.

**Hair as silhouette and era marker.** Hair changes the shape of the head, and the shape of the head is read instantly by the camera. A softly backlit halo of curls carries different energy than a sharp geometric cut; period hair signals decade before a single set piece appears in frame. The hair department is, in this sense, a second costume department — one operating on a canvas that the audience trusts implicitly because they assume it is real.

**Continuity as discipline.** The makeup-and-hair department maintains a continuity bible — detailed photographs, precise measurements, written notes on products and technique — because the scene shot on day sixty may cut to the scene shot on day three. Every minute element must be matched: the specific direction of a curl, the placement of a scar, the exact shade of lip. Continuity failure in makeup and hair is among the most distracting for an audience precisely because the human face is the thing they look at most.

## How this feeds the pipeline

Casting, costume, and makeup-and-hair are the three disciplines that answer the question: *who is this person, and what does their body tell us about them?* When the `character-sheet` skill is invoked, it captures exactly this triad — identity descriptor, wardrobe state, and HMU state — as the reference assets that survive into generation.

The casting principles here underwrite the identity descriptor: what kind of face, what quality of presence, what physical history the character carries. The costume principles underwrite the wardrobe block in the character sheet, including the color anchor that `guide-color-story.md` teaches you to assign deliberately. The makeup-and-hair principles underwrite the HMU state — the specific, reproducible description of skin, hair, and any practical effects that must remain consistent regardless of the order in which shots are generated.

Creative intent survives into the reference asset only when it has been made explicit. A casting instinct left as "she should feel weathered" will drift; translated into physical specifics — bone structure, skin texture, the particular quality of a face that has worked outdoors — it becomes a prompt that holds.
